During the Hour of Code event, everything ran smoothly. As Team 74, we worked hard to create an engaging and friendly environment for the children. Initially, they were shy and hesitant, but a few jokes and a warm approach helped break the ice.We began by asking if any of them had prior experience with similar courses. None of them had, which made the event even more interesting, as everyone was learning something new
We started with a brief introduction to programming. We discussed the general steps involved in programming and explained how complex software, like games and apps, is made up of small, relatively simple codes. In other words, we demistified coding. We also addressed a common misconception in computer science—that the focus should be on learning programming languages rather than on analytical thinking and problem-solving. We emphasized that coding is mainly about solving problems, and programming languages are just tools to help achieve that.
As recommended in instructions we used Classic Maze puzzle.We displayed the puzles on a projector so everyone could see the board and participate in problem-solving. First, we explained the meaning of each block, how they function, and how to connect them. To be completely honest, we simply asked what they thought those blocks meant, and they were able to explain blocks like 'irəli get' and 'sağa dön' accurately
After solving the first maze problem, we discussed what happens in the background, like what makes the character move forward when "Run" is pressed. We showed them the JavaScript code and briefly explained the difference between syntax and semantics. As we solved more problems, we introduced the concept of sequence in coding. We demonstrated this with the 3rd problem by intentionally placing the correct blocks in the wrong order to show the incorrect output. Then, we arranged the blocks correctly, highlighting that the sequence of commands is just as important as using the correct blocks.
Next, we introduced a more complex topic: loops. We explained what a loop is, gave real-life examples, and discussed how programmers use loops to avoid repetitive code. We also explained the difference between counted and conditional loops. Using the 7th and 18th problems in the Classic Maze, we demonstrated which type of loop is best suited for different tasks. In the 18th problem, we also introduced conditional statements, showing how conditions can influence decisions in both real life and programming. This exercise helped teach the basics of control flow.
The kids thoroughly enjoyed the event. Even though the session ended and their parents were waiting, many of them didn't want to leave and asked if we could come back for another session. Some of them even ran to their parants and said those teachers(pointing at us) tought us lots of things and they were genuinely excited and happy about the experience they gained. Their progress was impressive. We hadn't expected them to grasp everything so quickly. After a single explanation, they answered our questions with ease and solved challenging problems involving loops and conditions, like the 18th problem, through effective brainstorming

Our Community

As our community, we chose Baku Secondary School No. 242 for our event, where we engaged with a total of 33 enthusiastic 3rd-grade pupils. Their energy and active participation made the event a truly memorable experience. We would also like to extend our heartfelt thanks to the school's director, Sevinc Bayramova, and to teachers, Gunay Ehmedova and Beyim Guliyeva. Their support was invaluable. Teachers Gunay and Beyim kindly stepped in to help us manage the class when needed, while Ms. Bayramova graciously granted us permission to conduct the Hour of Code event at the school.
